Jakob Nielsen’s Usability Aspects

Usability is defined by 5 core aspects:
1. Ease of learning: How easy is it for users to accomplish basic tasks the first time they encounter the design?
2. Efficiency of use: Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they perform tasks?
3. Memorability: When users return to the design after a period of non-use, how easily can they reestablish proficiency?
4. Errors: How many errors do users make, how severe are these errors, and how easily can they recover?
5. Subjective satisfaction: How pleasant is it to use the design?

Common Usability Failures

Most modern interface issues fall into distinct categories:
• Information Overload: Failing to chunk related info (exceeding Miller's Law of 7±2 items).
• Inconsistent Styles: Random borders, typography, or layouts force users to constantly relearn patterns.
• Vague Controls: Text like "Click Here" or "maybe submit?" forces recall instead of simple recognition.
• Lack of Feedback: Not showing confirmation alerts or state changes, leaving users guessing.

Inspector's Observation: Complete challenges to generate usability insights. Most serious usability issues are the ones that stop users from completing their task.

Inspector's Conclusion

Identifying and fixing common usability problems systematically increases user satisfaction, reduces operational errors, and shortens the learning curve. A true Usability Inspector focuses on error prevention, visual consistency, and clear feedback to bridge the gap between user mental models and system states.
